## Quick Reference: Speech & Language Milestones

### Ages 12-18 Months
✅ Uses 10-20 words  
✅ Follows simple one-step commands ("Come here," "Give me")  
✅ Points to request or show interest  
✅ Imitates sounds and words  

🚩 **Refer if:**
- No words by 15-18 months
- Not responding to name
- Limited or no babbling

---

### Ages 18-24 Months
✅ Uses 50+ words by age 2  
✅ Combines two words ("more juice," "daddy go")  
✅ Follows two-step related directions  
✅ Asks simple questions ("What's that?")  

🚩 **Refer if:**
- Fewer than 25 words by 24 months
- Not combining words by 24 months
- Frequent frustration with communication
- Regression in language skills

---

### Ages 2-3 Years
✅ 200-300 word vocabulary  
✅ Uses 3-4 word sentences  
✅ 50-75% intelligible to strangers  
✅ Asks "why" and "what" questions  

🚩 **Refer if:**
- Limited vocabulary (<100 words)
- Speech mostly unintelligible
- Relies heavily on gestures
- Omits most beginning/ending sounds

---

### Ages 3-4 Years
✅ Uses 4-5 word sentences with grammar  
✅ 75-90% intelligible to unfamiliar listeners  
✅ Tells simple stories  
✅ Asks and answers questions  

🚩 **Refer if:**
- Consistently omits or substitutes sounds (e.g., "tat" for "cat," "wabbit" for "rabbit")
- Strangers cannot understand child
- Difficulty with back-and-forth conversation
- Stuttering with tension, blocks, or repetitions

---

### Ages 4-5 Years
✅ Uses complex sentences  
✅ 90-100% intelligible  
✅ Tells detailed stories with sequence  
✅ Masters most speech sounds (except /r/, /s/, /l/, /th/)  

🚩 **Refer if:**
- Persistent articulation errors beyond developmental norms
- Difficulty with narrative skills
- Struggles socially due to communication challenges
- Stuttering lasting >6 months

---

### Ages 5-7 Years (School-Age)
✅ Masters all speech sounds by age 7-8  
✅ Uses grammar correctly  
✅ Understands and uses abstract language  
✅ Reads and writes at grade level  

🚩 **Refer if:**
- Persistent lisp or /r/ distortion
- Reading/writing delays tied to language deficits
- Difficulty following multi-step directions
- Social communication challenges
- Voice quality concerns (chronic hoarseness)

---

## Common Reasons for Referral

### ✅ Articulation/Phonology
- Sound substitutions or omissions beyond developmental age
- Inconsistent or distorted sound productions
- Intelligibility concerns

### ✅ Expressive Language
- Limited vocabulary
- Short/telegraphic sentences
- Difficulty organizing thoughts or telling stories
- Word-finding difficulties

### ✅ Receptive Language
- Doesn't follow age-appropriate directions
- Struggles to understand questions
- Difficulty with inferencing or abstract language

### ✅ Fluency (Stuttering)
- Sound/syllable repetitions (b-b-b-ball)
- Prolongations (sssssnake)
- Blocks (getting stuck with no sound)
- Physical tension or secondary behaviors
- Stuttering >6 months

### ✅ Voice
- Chronic hoarseness or breathiness
- Vocal strain or fatigue
- Loss of voice by end of day
- Pitch or volume concerns

### ✅ Social Communication (Pragmatics)
- Difficulty with turn-taking in conversation
- Doesn't read social cues
- Talks "at" people, not "with" them
- Challenges making/keeping friends due to communication

### ✅ Feeding/Swallowing (Pediatric)
- Oral motor difficulties
- Picky eating related to oral sensory issues
- Prolonged feeding times
- Coughing/choking during meals

## Why Early Referral Matters

Research shows that **early intervention (ages 0-5) leads to significantly better outcomes** than waiting until school-age. The earlier a child receives support, the faster they progress.

**Key reasons to refer early:**
- Critical brain development windows (ages 0-5)
- Prevents secondary issues (behavior, social challenges, academic delays)
- Reduces long-term need for intensive services
- Builds parent confidence and skills

**"Wait and see" risks:**
- Gaps widen over time
- Self-esteem and behavior issues emerge
- Literacy challenges in elementary school
- More intensive (and costly) intervention needed later
