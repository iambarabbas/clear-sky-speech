What is Speech Therapy?

Speech therapy (also called speech-language therapy) is a specialized treatment that helps children improve their communication skills. Despite the name, it addresses much more than just "speech"—it encompasses language development, social communication, reading readiness, and feeding challenges.

A licensed speech-language pathologist (SLP) evaluates your child's communication abilities and creates a personalized treatment plan to target specific goals.

What Speech Therapy Can Help With:

  • Articulation/Sound Production: Difficulty pronouncing certain sounds (like "r," "s," or "th")
  • Expressive Language: Trouble putting words together into sentences or expressing thoughts
  • Receptive Language: Difficulty understanding what others say or following directions
  • Stuttering/Fluency: Repetitions, blocks, or prolonged sounds that disrupt speech flow
  • Voice Disorders: Chronic hoarseness, breathiness, or vocal strain
  • Social Communication: Challenges with conversation skills, understanding social cues, or pragmatic language
  • Feeding/Swallowing: Oral motor difficulties affecting eating or drinking
  • Early Language Delays: Late talkers or children with limited vocabulary for their age

When Should My Child Start Speech Therapy?

One of the most common questions we hear from Park City parents is: "Should I wait and see if my child outgrows this, or should I get help now?"

Here's the truth: early intervention makes a significant difference. Research consistently shows that children who receive speech therapy between ages 2-5 make faster progress and have better long-term outcomes than those who wait.

Signs Your Child May Benefit from Speech Therapy:

Ages 18 Months - 2 Years:

  • Not using any words by 18 months
  • Fewer than 50 words by age 2
  • Not combining two words together ("more juice," "bye-bye daddy") by 24 months
  • Difficulty imitating sounds or words
  • Mostly using gestures instead of words to communicate

Ages 2-3 Years:

  • Limited vocabulary compared to peers
  • Difficult to understand by family members (less than 50% intelligible)
  • Not putting 3-4 words together into sentences
  • Doesn't follow simple two-step directions ("Get your shoes and put them by the door")
  • Doesn't ask questions or engage in back-and-forth conversation

Ages 3-5 Years:

  • Strangers can't understand your child's speech (less than 75% intelligible by age 3, less than 90% by age 4)
  • Substituting or omitting multiple sounds ("tat" for "cat," "boo" for "blue")
  • Short, simple sentences when peers are using longer, more complex language
  • Difficulty staying on topic or participating in conversations
  • Avoids talking or gets frustrated when trying to communicate
  • Frequent stuttering that lasts more than 6 months

School-Age (5+ Years):

  • Still mispronouncing sounds that peers say correctly
  • Struggles with reading or spelling (language skills are foundational for literacy)
  • Difficulty organizing thoughts or telling stories in sequence
  • Misunderstands questions or instructions at school
  • Social challenges due to communication difficulties

Trust your instincts. If you're concerned about your child's communication development, it's worth getting a professional evaluation. Speech therapy is not just for "severe" cases—it's designed to help children at all levels reach their full potential.

Speech Therapy Options in Park City

Park City families have several options for accessing speech therapy services. Understanding the differences can help you choose what's best for your child and family.

1. School-Based Speech Therapy (Free, IEP/504 Plans)

What it is: Public schools are required to provide speech therapy services to children who qualify for special education under an Individualized Education Program (IEP) or 504 plan.

Who qualifies: Only children whose speech/language difficulties impact their educational performance. The bar is relatively high—a child must demonstrate that their communication challenges interfere with learning.

Pros:

  • Free to families
  • Services provided during the school day
  • SLP collaborates with teachers

Cons:

  • Focus is on educational needs, not all communication goals
  • High caseloads (some school SLPs serve 50-70+ students)
  • Limited session frequency (often 1-2x per week for 20-30 minutes)
  • Group sessions are common
  • Not available during summer or school breaks
  • Parents typically not involved in sessions

Best for: Children with moderate to severe needs whose challenges primarily affect school performance.

2. Hospital/Clinic-Based Therapy (Insurance-Based)

What it is: Speech therapy provided by hospital systems like Intermountain Healthcare or University of Utah Health, typically billed through insurance.

Pros:

  • Covered by most insurance plans (subject to deductibles/copays)
  • Access to multidisciplinary teams if needed
  • Available year-round

Cons:

  • Often requires referrals from pediatricians
  • Waitlists can be 2-4+ months in Summit County
  • Limited appointment times (often must fit into traditional business hours)
  • Insurance may limit number of sessions approved
  • Therapist changes are common due to turnover
  • Travel required (nearest Intermountain locations in Heber City or Salt Lake)

Best for: Families with strong insurance coverage who can wait for an opening and travel to appointments.

3. Private Practice Speech Therapy (Private Pay)

What it is: Independent speech-language pathologists who operate their own practices and do not bill insurance directly. Families pay out-of-pocket.

Pros:

  • No waitlists—typically can start within 1-2 weeks
  • Flexible scheduling (evenings, weekends, teletherapy options)
  • Personalized, individualized treatment plans
  • Longer session times if beneficial (45-60 minutes)
  • Consistent therapist—no turnover or reassignments
  • Parent coaching and involvement in every session
  • Treatment addresses family priorities, not just educational goals
  • Many provide superbills for potential insurance reimbursement

Cons:

  • Out-of-pocket cost (typically $100-200 per session)
  • May require submitting superbills to insurance for partial reimbursement

Best for: Families who value flexibility, personalized care, and want to start therapy quickly without navigating insurance or school bureaucracy.

How Much Does Speech Therapy Cost in Park City?

Cost is one of the biggest factors parents consider when seeking speech therapy. Here's what to expect in the Park City area:

Initial Evaluation:

  • School-based: Free
  • Insurance-based clinics: $0-300 (depending on deductible/copay)
  • Private pay: $200-350

Ongoing Therapy Sessions:

  • School-based: Free
  • Insurance-based clinics: $25-75 per session (copay), subject to annual session limits
  • Private pay: $100-200 per 45-60 minute session

Is Private Pay Worth It?

Many Park City families are surprised to learn that private pay can actually be more cost-effective than navigating insurance, especially when you factor in:

  • Time savings: No waiting months for an opening; start therapy when your child needs it
  • Progress efficiency: Personalized 1:1 sessions often mean faster progress (fewer total sessions needed)
  • Convenience: Flexible scheduling reduces missed work and school time
  • Consistency: Same therapist throughout treatment builds trust and accelerates progress

Additionally, many private pay SLPs provide superbills—detailed receipts you can submit to your insurance company for out-of-network reimbursement. Check your plan's out-of-network benefits; some families get 50-80% reimbursed.

What to Expect During Speech Therapy

If you've never been to speech therapy before, here's what a typical experience looks like:

Step 1: Initial Evaluation (60-90 minutes)

The first session is an assessment to understand your child's strengths and challenges. The SLP will:

  • Interview you about developmental history, concerns, and family priorities
  • Observe your child's communication in play and conversation
  • Administer standardized tests to measure specific skills
  • Evaluate oral motor function (if relevant)
  • Provide a summary of findings and recommendations

At the end, you'll receive a diagnosis (if applicable) and a proposed treatment plan.

Step 2: Treatment Planning

Together with your SLP, you'll establish specific, measurable goals such as:

  • "Max will produce the /r/ sound correctly in words and sentences with 80% accuracy"
  • "Ella will use 3-4 word sentences to request items during play with minimal prompting"
  • "Liam will reduce stuttering behaviors by using smooth, easy speech techniques"

You'll also decide on frequency (typically 1-2 times per week) and session length.

Step 3: Ongoing Therapy Sessions (30-60 minutes)

Therapy sessions are play-based for young children and activity-based for older kids. The SLP uses games, books, crafts, and interactive tasks to practice targeted skills.

Each session includes:

  • Warm-up: Building rapport and reviewing home practice
  • Targeted practice: Working on specific goals with high repetition and feedback
  • Generalization activities: Practicing skills in natural conversation or play
  • Parent coaching: Showing you strategies to support progress at home

Step 4: Progress Monitoring & Adjustments

Every 6-8 weeks, your SLP will formally measure progress and adjust goals as needed. Communication is ongoing—you should always feel informed about what your child is working on and how you can help at home.

Step 5: Discharge

Therapy ends when your child reaches their goals or plateaus in progress. The SLP will provide a summary and recommendations for continued practice.

Finding the Right Speech Therapist in Park City

Not all speech therapists are the same. Here's what to look for:

Credentials to Verify:

  • CCC-SLP: Certificate of Clinical Competence from the American Speech-Language-Hearing Association (ASHA)
  • State licensure: Licensed in Utah
  • Master's degree: All practicing SLPs must have at least a master's degree

Questions to Ask Potential Therapists:

  1. "What experience do you have with my child's specific challenge?" (e.g., apraxia, autism, stuttering)
  2. "What does a typical session look like?"
  3. "How do you involve parents in therapy?"
  4. "How often do you recommend sessions?"
  5. "What are your cancellation and payment policies?"
  6. "Do you provide superbills for insurance reimbursement?"

Red Flags:

  • Not willing to answer questions about credentials or approach
  • Promises unrealistic timelines ("Your child will be fixed in 6 weeks")
  • Doesn't involve parents or provide home practice activities
  • Keeps extending therapy indefinitely without clear progress

Tips for Supporting Your Child at Home

Speech therapy works best when families practice strategies at home. Here are some universal tips:

1. Talk, Talk, Talk

Narrate your day. "I'm putting the red cup on the table." "Let's wash your hands with soap." The more language exposure, the better.

2. Follow Your Child's Lead

Let your child choose activities during play. When they're engaged, they're more motivated to communicate.

3. Expand Their Sentences

If your child says "Car go," you respond with "Yes, the blue car is going fast!" This models the next level without correcting.

4. Read Together Daily

Books are language goldmines. Ask questions, make predictions, and talk about the pictures.

5. Limit Screen Time

Passive screen time doesn't build communication skills. Prioritize face-to-face interaction.

6. Practice Speech Sounds in Natural Moments

If your child is working on the /k/ sound, emphasize it during daily routines: "Let's get your coat and shoes. Time for car!"

7. Celebrate Effort, Not Just Accuracy

Praise your child for trying, even if the word isn't perfect yet. "I love how hard you're working on that /r/ sound!"

Common Questions Park City Parents Ask

Q: Will my child always have a speech problem?

A: Most children who receive speech therapy make significant improvements and many "graduate" from therapy completely. The earlier you start, the better the outcomes. Some challenges (like certain language-based learning disabilities or stuttering) may require longer-term support, but therapy equips kids with strategies to manage successfully.

Q: Can teletherapy really work?

A: Yes! Research shows teletherapy is just as effective as in-person therapy for most speech and language goals. It's especially convenient for busy Park City families and eliminates travel time. Many kids actually focus better in their own home environment.

Q: My pediatrician said to "wait and see." Should I?

A: If you're concerned, get an evaluation. Even if your pediatrician isn't worried, you know your child best. A speech-language evaluation provides objective data and peace of mind. Early intervention is always better than waiting until challenges become more entrenched.

Q: How long will my child need therapy?

A: It varies widely. Some children achieve their goals in 3-6 months; others need 1-2 years. Factors include severity, frequency of sessions, home practice consistency, and individual learning pace. Your SLP will give you a realistic timeline after the initial evaluation.

Q: What if my child refuses to cooperate during therapy?

A: Skilled pediatric SLPs are trained to work with reluctant or shy children. Therapy is play-based, engaging, and builds on your child's interests. Most kids warm up quickly and actually look forward to sessions.
